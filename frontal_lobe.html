Frontal Lobe

  • The frontal lobes are located directly behind the forehead.
  • The frontal lobes are the largest lobes in the human brain and they are also the most common region of injury in traumatic brain injury.
  • The frontal lobes are important for voluntary movement, expressive language and for managing higher level executive functions.
  • It is responsible for maintaining optimal mood, behaviour and social judgement.
  • Mental illnesses affecting the frontal lobe can include:
  • Mania, Depression, Confabulation, Catatonia, Perservation, Obsessive compulsions and Schizophrenia

Mania

  • Mania is a psychological condition that causes a person to experience unreasonable euphoria, very intense moods, hyperactivity, and delusions.
  • Mania is a common symptom of bipolar disorder.
  • Can be a dangerous condition for several reasons - people may not sleep or eat while in a manic epsiode, they may engage in risky behaviours and harm themselves.
  • People with mania have a greater risk of experienceing hallucinations and other perceptual disturbances.

What causes mania?

  • Family history may play a factor in mania.
  • Some people are prone to mania or manic episodes because of an underlying medical condition or psychiatric illness, such as bipolar disorder.
  • Brain scans to show that some patients with mania have slightly different brain structures or activity.
  • Environmental changes can trigger mania (stressful life events, financial stress, relationships, illness etc.)

What are the symptoms of mania?

  • Extreme excitement and euphoria as well as other intense moods
  • Hyperactiveness, they may experience hallucinations or delusions
  • Some patients feel jumpy and extremely anxious
  • Quick change in the mood - from manic to depressive, with extremely low energy levels
  • 'Speed up' body system
  • Racing thoughts and rapid speech
  • Lack of sleep, poor work performance
  • Aggressive behaviour, durg or alcohol abuse

How is mania treated?

  • Hospitalisation can be necessary if a patient's mania is severe or is accompanied by psychosis - it can help a patient from njuring themselves.
  • Medications are typically the first line of mania treatment (aim to balance a patient's mood and reduce the risk of self-injury).
  • !!Medications should be used only as prescribed by a medical professional!!
  • Psychotheraphy sessions can help a patient identify mania triggers and manage stress.

Tips for coping with a manic episode

  • Reach out to your healthcare team
  • Identify medications that help
  • Avoid triggers that worsen your mania
  • Maintain a regular eating and sleeping schedule
  • Watch your finance
  • Set up daily reminders
